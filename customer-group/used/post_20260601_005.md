Losses are not failures. They are the cost of doing business.

This is a difficult concept to accept. We are wired to view losses as bad, as something to be avoided, as evidence that we made a mistake. But in trading, losses are inevitable. No trader wins every trade. The best traders in the world lose 40-50% of the time.

The difference is that professional traders expect losses. They plan for them. They accept them as part of the process. Amateur traders are surprised by losses, which causes them to react emotionally and make poor decisions.

Think about a restaurant. Every month, the restaurant loses money on some customers. Some meals are sent back. Some ingredients spoil. Some staff make mistakes. These losses are built into the business model. The restaurant owner does not close down because one meal was returned. They focus on the overall profitability of the month, the quarter, the year.

Trading works the same way. Individual trades do not matter. What matters is the collective result of all your trades over time.

The problem is that losses feel personal. They trigger our ego. We want to be right. We want to win. When a trade goes against us, it feels like the market is attacking us personally. This feeling causes us to deviate from our plan, to chase losses, to make irrational decisions.

The professional trader separates their identity from their trades. A losing trade does not mean they are a bad trader. It simply means that trade did not work out. They review it, learn from it, and move on.

This detachment is not easy to develop. It takes time and practice. But it is essential for long-term success.

One helpful perspective: track your trades in batches of 20 or 30 rather than individually. Look at the overall performance of the batch. Did you follow your plan? Did you manage risk properly? Did the batch produce a positive result? This shifts your focus from individual outcomes to process and consistency.

Losses are tuition. They are the price you pay to learn, to improve, to eventually master your craft. The trader who cannot handle losses will not survive long enough to experience the wins.
