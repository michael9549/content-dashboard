A single trade should never have the power to end your trading career.

This sounds obvious. Yet traders violate this principle every day. They risk too much on one setup. They add to losing positions. They remove stop losses because they are convinced the trade will work out.

Risk management is not a separate part of trading. It is the foundation. Without it, no strategy matters. No edge matters. No amount of market knowledge matters.

Think of your trading account as a business. Businesses do not bet the entire company on one customer, one product, or one decision. They diversify. They limit exposure. They plan for things to go wrong because they know things will go wrong.

Your trading account deserves the same respect.

The math of risk management is simple but brutal. If you lose 50% of your account, you need to make 100% just to get back to even. If you lose 80%, you need to make 400%. The deeper the hole, the harder the climb.

This is why professional traders think in terms of survival first and profits second. They know that staying in the game is more important than any single trade. They know that small, consistent gains compound over time into significant returns.

The amateur trader asks: How much can I make on this trade?
The professional trader asks: How much can I lose if this trade goes wrong?

This shift in perspective changes everything.

Risk management is not about being pessimistic. It is about being realistic. The market does not owe you profits. It does not care about your goals or your timeline. It simply presents opportunities, and your job is to take the good ones while protecting yourself from the bad ones.

A good rule of thumb: never risk more than 1-2% of your account on a single trade. This means you can have ten losing trades in a row and still have 80-90% of your capital. You can survive a bad streak. You can learn from it. You can come back stronger.

The trader who risks 10% per trade only needs a few losses to be crippled. They do not get the chance to learn. They do not get the chance to improve. They are out of the game before they ever had a real shot.

What percentage of your account do you currently risk per trade, and is it sustainable through a losing streak?
