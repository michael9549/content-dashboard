Ira Glass, the radio host, once described something every creative person experiences. He called it 'the gap.'

When you start any creative work, you have taste. You know what good looks like. You can recognize excellence. But your ability to produce that excellence doesn't match your ability to recognize it yet.

So there's a gap. Between what you want to create and what you're actually creating. Between your taste and your skill.

Most people never close that gap. They quit in the middle, convinced they'll never be good enough.

But the ones who make it... they do something simple. They keep creating. They produce a volume of work. They close the gap through repetition, through failure, through showing up even when the work disappoints them.

Trading has the same gap.

You can see what good trading looks like. You can recognize a well-executed plan, a patient entry, a disciplined exit. But when you try to do it yourself, something gets lost in translation.

Your emotions get in the way. You hesitate. You rush. You deviate from the plan. The gap stares back at you in your trading journal.

The only way to close it is volume. Repetition. Showing up trade after trade, even when you know you're not executing at the level you want to be.

Every professional trader you admire once stood exactly where you're standing. They just refused to leave until the gap closed.

How much volume are you willing to put in?
