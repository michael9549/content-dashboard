Jacob Riis, a social reformer, once described the stonecutter as a metaphor for persistence.

The stonecutter hammers away at a rock a hundred times without as much as a crack showing. Then, on the hundred and first blow, the rock splits in two.

It wasn't the final blow that did it. It was all the blows that came before.

Trading can feel like that stonecutter's hammer. Day after day. Week after week. Sometimes month after month. Working. Learning. Practicing. And the results don't seem to match the effort.

It's easy to believe the work isn't working. To think the strategy is wrong. To chase the next thing that promises faster results.

But the stonecutter knows something important: the rock always splits. It has to. Physics demands it. The accumulated force of all those blows eventually overcomes the stone's resistance.

Your accumulated effort is doing the same thing. Every chart you study. Every trade you review. Every mistake you learn from. It's all force. All accumulating. All preparing for the moment the rock splits.

The traders who make it aren't necessarily the most talented. They're the ones who keep swinging the hammer when there's no crack in sight.

How many more swings are you willing to take?
