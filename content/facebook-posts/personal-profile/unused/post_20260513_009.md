I came across a straightforward three-step closing system from Andy Elliott, and the simplicity of it really highlights why so many salespeople struggle to close deals.

Most salespeople overcomplicate the close. They keep talking, keep pitching, keep trying to convince. The result is they talk themselves right out of the sale.

The timeline of a deal is simple: you open, you work through the middle, and you close. Most people focus on everything except what actually gets the deal done.

**Step One: Take Control Immediately**

You have to take control within the first two to three seconds. Not minutes. Seconds.

When you meet someone, your energy, your tonality, your voice, your body language all communicate whether you're in charge or not. Welcome them like they're entering your home. Show massive amounts of love and enthusiasm. Make them feel genuinely welcomed.

If you walk up weak, hesitant, or unsure, they'll interrupt you and take control. Once they have control, you've already lost.

Taking control isn't about being aggressive. It's about being confident, welcoming, and leading the conversation from the very first moment.

**Step Two: Surface the Dominant Buying Motive**

After you establish control, you need to ask great questions. This is your fact-finding, intelligence-gathering phase. Your goal is to surface their dominant buying motive.

What does dominant mean? It's the number one thing they need. Not want. Need.

Someone trading in a car might want better gas mileage, lower miles, a warranty, and a smoother ride. All those things are great. But their dominant buying motive might be: "We're having another kid. We have three kids, we're having a fourth. We're in a car. We need a third row. It's not optional. We have to have one."

That's the dominant motive. Everything else is secondary. When you understand this, you can sell all the other features, but you always anchor back to what matters most.

People have problems. You want to make every sale? Find people's problems and solve them. It's that simple. But you can't solve what you don't understand, which is why asking great questions is critical.

When you have control and ask great questions, people will happily answer. They want someone to understand their situation.

**Step Three: Close and Circle Back**

After you've established control and surfaced their dominant buying motive, you ask for their business. You're excited. You're confident. You believe this is the perfect solution for them.

You ask for the business. Maybe you say: "If I could get this deal to your satisfaction, would you want to take it home?" Or: "How would you like to title this, in your name or both names?"

Now here's where most salespeople fall apart. When the prospect says no, they panic. They start overselling. They get desperate. They can smell the weakness.

The right response is to overcome the objection. Use your objection-handling skills. But if they say no a second time, stop. Don't ask for their business again. Don't get desperate. Don't start discounting or throwing in extras.

Instead, circle back.

Go back to your questioning phase. Remind them why they're there. Surface all the information they gave you and sell it back to them in 30 seconds.

"You know what, I totally understand. The reason I showed you this vehicle is because when you came in, you told me you've got a new little one on the way. In two or three months, you'll need that extra seat. It's not safe without it. We can't put the baby on someone's lap. So it's not a matter of if you need to buy a car, it's just when. And the when is when the deal's right."

"You guys thought about what numbers needed to look like at the kitchen table. You came in today and told me X, Y, and Z was important. This vehicle has X, Y, and Z. So let me ask: if I could save you some time and money, and the vehicle has everything you want, would that offend you? If I could save you time and money, would that upset you?"

"No? Great. Let me show you. In the end, it's your decision. Fair enough?"

**Why This Works**

Before someone buys, they often forget why they're there. They remember they're about to spend money. They get nervous. Your job is to remind them of their own reasoning.

You surface their dominant buying motive in step two. When you circle back in step three, you're simply reminding them of what they already told you. You're not convincing them of anything new. You're just reconnecting them with their own stated needs.

"You went through all the thought processes at home. You made it this far because you wanted X, Y, and Z. This vehicle is X, Y, and Z. You're not just buying from somebody, you're buying from me. I'll give you world-class service. It's not a matter of if you're going to buy, it's when. And the when is when the deal is right."

**The Bottom Line**

One, take control in the first two to three seconds. Two, surface the dominant buying motive through great questions. Three, ask for the business, overcome objections, and if they say no twice, circle back to what they told you they needed.

Don't oversell. Don't get desperate. Don't come from weakness. Hold control. Remind them why they're there. Make it easy to say yes.
