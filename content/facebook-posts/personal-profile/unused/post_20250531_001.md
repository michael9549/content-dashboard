The most dangerous thing you can believe is that you are not being manipulated.

Chase Hughes said something in a recent podcast that stopped me cold. He said anybody who thinks they are not susceptible to a psyop is completely susceptible to a psyop. And he is right. The people who think they are too smart to get played are usually the easiest marks.

Here is what changed everything for him. After years of studying psychology, neuroanatomy, brain science, and working as a trial consultant, he finally reached a point where no matter what is on the news, his brain registers it all as theater. Not some of it. All of it.

Think about that. If politics was actually about results, it would not require cameras. It would not need performance. They would just do the work and move on. But that is not what we get. We get a show. A season finale every four years. And the moment you start feeling excited about some political win, some part of your brain should smack that down and remind you: you are watching a play.

But this is not just about politics. This is about perspective. And perspective is the single most powerful tool you have.

Chase talked about how shifting perspective fixes PTSD. That is literally the mechanism. People do deep psychedelic journeys and come back saying their PTSD is gone. Nothing changed in their past. The trauma still happened. But their perspective shifted. They zoomed out. And that one fiber of the carpet they were staring at suddenly was not the whole world anymore.

He applies this to parenting in a way that blew my mind. He taught his kids two questions about every person they encounter.

First: what does that person want me to think about them?

Not what are they saying. What do they want me to think? The guy in the airport with the loud clothes is not saying "I need attention." He is saying something deeper. He is afraid of being ignored. Afraid of loneliness. And when you start seeing people is behavior through that lens, you cannot help but have empathy.

Second question: what would they be afraid of if that were true?

The person seeking attention. The politician performing on stage. The friend posting constantly about their perfect life. What are they actually afraid of? Usually it is the same thing you are afraid of. Isolation. Irrelevance. Being seen as a fraud.

And here is the beautiful part. When you start asking these questions, you stop seeing enemies. You start seeing people. Scared people. Hurt people. People playing out story archetypes they do not even know they are in.

Chase mentioned something else that hit hard. He said the beginning lie that evil has to sell everyone starts with separation. If I want you to hate someone, I have to convince you that they are not you. Because if we are all one, if we are all connected, hatred becomes impossible. You are just hurting yourself.

This is why the system pushes division constantly. Left vs right. This group vs that group. It is all theater designed to keep you from zooming out and seeing the bigger picture.

So here is my question for you. Where in your life are you zoomed in too tight on one fiber of the carpet? Where are you taking something so seriously that it is causing you suffering? And what would change if you just grabbed the camera, pulled back, and looked at it from a higher angle?

The answers are usually simpler than we make them. But you have to be willing to shift your perspective first.
