# Steve Jobs on Managing People: The Best Ideas Must Win

This is a short clip but it contains one of the most important leadership principles I have ever heard. Steve Jobs is being interviewed about how he manages people at Apple, and his answer is not what most people expect.

He says if you want to hire great people and have them stay working for you, you have to let them make a lot of decisions. You have to be run by ideas, not hierarchy. The best ideas have to win. Otherwise good people do not stay.

Think about how rare this is in practice. Most companies are run by hierarchy. The boss has the final say. The person with the highest title wins the argument. The new employee with a brilliant idea gets shut down because they have not been there long enough or they are not in the right department.

Jobs says explicitly that he does not win all the arguments. He says he wishes he did but he cannot. Because if the best ideas do not win, the best people leave. It is that simple.

The interviewer asks if he is more than just a facilitator who runs meetings. Jobs says of course he contributes ideas. Why would he be there if he did not? But the key is that his ideas have to compete on equal ground with everyone else ideas. There is no automatic win because he is the CEO.

This is what separates companies that attract top talent from companies that constantly lose their best people. Top performers do not stay where their ideas are ignored. They do not stay where politics matter more than merit. They do not stay where the boss ego is more important than the best solution.

The other part of the clip that stands out is when Jobs talks about having wonderful arguments. He does not say they have friendly discussions or productive meetings. He says arguments. Real disagreement. Passionate debate about what is right. Not personal attacks. Not politics. Just a battle of ideas where the strongest one survives.

Most leaders are uncomfortable with this. They want consensus. They want everyone to get along. They interpret disagreement as disrespect. So they surround themselves with yes people who tell them what they want to hear. And slowly the company loses its edge because nobody is pushing back on bad ideas anymore.

Jobs approach requires two things that are hard to maintain. First, you need the confidence to be wrong in public. Most leaders are terrified of this. They think admitting they lost an argument weakens their authority. Jobs saw it differently. He thought it strengthened the culture. If the CEO can lose an argument gracefully, everyone else can too.

Second, you need to hire people who are actually smarter than you in specific areas. Most leaders are also terrified of this. They hire people who are good but not too good. People who will not outshine them. Jobs did the opposite. He hired world class talent and then gave them the freedom to prove he was wrong when he was wrong.

The result speaks for itself. Apple under Jobs created products that changed multiple industries. Not because Jobs had all the answers. But because he built a culture where the best answer could come from anywhere.

The practical question for anyone running a team is this. When was the last time you changed your mind because someone on your team convinced you that you were wrong? If you cannot remember, you might be running a hierarchy instead of an idea meritocracy. And if you are, your best people are already thinking about leaving.

How do you handle disagreement on your team? Do the best ideas actually win, or does the highest title always decide?
