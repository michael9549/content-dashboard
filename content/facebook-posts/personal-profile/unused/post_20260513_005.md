I came across some insights from Tony Robbins about what it really takes to grow a business, and his perspective on timing, leverage, and raving fans really resonated with me.

Most entrepreneurs are working hard but working wrong. They're doing the right things at the wrong time, and they don't understand why they're stuck.

**Understanding the Seasons of Business**

Think about human development. An infant operates differently than a toddler. A teenager operates differently than either of them. You wouldn't give car keys to a toddler, and you wouldn't treat a teenager like a baby.

Businesses go through similar stages of development. The problem is that most entrepreneurs approach every stage the same way. They use toddler strategies on teenage problems.

If you plant in winter, it doesn't matter how much you believe or how hard you work. Nothing will grow. You have to understand the timing of what's happening in your industry and in your specific company.

**The Founder's Trap**

Here's a pattern that traps most business owners. They start the business. They do everything themselves because nobody can do it as well as they can. They let people help, but then they criticize how it's done. They end up redoing everything.

Sound familiar?

This is the Founder's Trap. You can't grow because there's no leverage. You're the bottleneck. The only way out is to start developing other people, even though they won't do things exactly like you would.

**Good Judgment Comes From Experience**

Success in business comes down to one thing: good judgment. Good decisions lead to success. The problem is that good decisions are the result of experience, and experience often comes from bad decisions.

You have to let people fail and still support them, or you'll never have a business that scales. If you try to prevent every mistake, it's already over. The tempo of business won't allow you to move forward without some errors.

The key is learning faster than other people. If you can recognize patterns and use them, you'll dominate your market.

**The Three Ways to Grow Any Business**

There are three fundamental ways to grow any business:

First, know your ideal customer with precision. You should know more about them than they know about themselves. Who will stay with you when the economy crashes? Who loves your product enough to tell everyone else about it?

Life is the dance between what you desire most and what you fear most. If you understand both for your customers, you understand their wounds and their opportunities. You know how to deliver for them.

Second, create an irresistible offer. You need something so incredibly good that people think they'd be an idiot not to at least try it. When you have an irresistible offer, you win.

Third, over-deliver. Do more than you promise. If you combine an irresistible offer with over-delivery, and you truly understand your customer's deepest needs, you'll build something incredible.

**The Energy Factor**

Energy equals profit. The more engaged your team members are, the more profitable your company becomes. Every statistic shows this.

Notice how when your energy is low, you become more negative. Your mind finds all the problems. When your energy is high, you just solve things. There are lots of moving parts in any business, and you need energy to manage them.

**The Gladiator Mindset**

Business is the ultimate spiritual game. To succeed, you have to be a gladiator. The odds are against you. Fifty percent of businesses fail in the first year. The more you play, the more likely you are to fail statistically.

But gladiators don't care about the odds. They say: "Everyone else might die today, but not me. It doesn't matter what shows up. It doesn't matter what people say. It doesn't matter who my competition is. I will find the way or make the way."

**Innovation and Marketing**

If you had to reduce business to two things, they would be innovation and marketing.

Innovation means finding a better way to meet your customers' needs. That might mean faster, quicker, easier. It might mean cheaper. It might mean more expensive because it meets their need for quality or significance.

People buy for different reasons. Some value variety. Some value certainty. Some want to feel special and unique. Some value connection. Some want growth. Part of innovating is meeting their specific needs better than anyone else.

Marketing is getting people to want to do business with you. To seek you out. To find you.

If you innovate and market effectively, you have a company like Apple. They kept making better products, but they're also among the best marketers.

**The Raving Fan Strategy**

The ultimate barrier is building clients that are raving fans. When Apple almost went under, it wasn't their technology that saved them. It was their raving fans. People like Robbins' team who refused to switch to IBM or Microsoft because Apple had been so good to them for so long.

Your goal is to get to that point. But to do that, you have to be the gladiator. You have to face every challenge and keep adding value.

The way you get wealthy is by doing more for others than anybody else in the marketplace, over and over again. You build a brand. When you have a brand, people reach past other options to get to you.

But to get there, you have to fall in love with your clients, not your product. Your product has a short lifespan today. Your relationship with your clients is what endures.
