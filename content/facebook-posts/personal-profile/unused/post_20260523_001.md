Raymond Loewy designed the 20th century. The 1953 Studebaker. The Pennsylvania Railroad locomotive. The modern Greyhound bus. The Air Force One livery that still flies today. The Exxon logo. That egg-shaped pencil sharpener in every classroom.

But his most important creation wasn't a product. It was a theory: MAYA. Most Advanced Yet Acceptable.

Loewy understood something that most marketers, salespeople, and entrepreneurs still struggle with today. People are torn between two opposing forces: neophilia (our love of new things) and neophobia (our fear of anything too new).

We say we want innovation. We crave the latest thing. But the research tells a different story.

Spotify learned this the hard way. When they built their "Discover Weekly" feature, they initially programmed it to deliver completely new music. Songs you'd never heard. Artists you didn't know. Pure novelty. And engagement tanked.

Then a bug slipped through. The algorithm accidentally let some familiar songs through. Some artists people already knew. They fixed the bug quickly, but when they tested the "pure" version again, engagement plummeted even further.

That little bit of familiarity made the discovery platform work.

Harvard researchers found the same pattern in academic research. They created 150 dummy research proposals and asked scientists to score them. The proposals that got funded weren't the most novel. They weren't the most familiar either. They were "optimally familiar." Advanced yet acceptable.

Here's why this matters for anyone selling anything:

When you're pitching a new product, a new service, a new idea, your instinct is to emphasize how different you are. How revolutionary. How unlike anything else on the market. But that's exactly what triggers resistance.

The human brain is an organ of ancient chemistry. For most of human history, unfamiliar plants and animals were dangerous. If you recognized something and it hadn't killed you yet, that was a very good sign. Familiarity meant safety.

This is why sequels outsell original movies. Why we listen to new songs but prefer familiar chord structures. Why people prefer their mirror image (seen thousands of times) over photographs (seen rarely).

The most successful products don't feel new. They feel familiar the first time you see them.

Steve Jobs understood this. The iPhone wasn't introduced as a revolutionary new device. It was "three devices in one": an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator. All familiar concepts. The revolution was hidden inside the familiar packaging.

When you're selling something surprising, you have to make it familiar. When you're selling something familiar, you have to make it surprising.

This applies to your marketing, your sales calls, your product development, even how you position yourself in the market.

If you have something truly new, don't lead with the novelty. Lead with the familiar problem it solves. The recognizable pain point. The well-understood frustration. Then introduce your solution as the logical next step.

If you're in a crowded market with familiar solutions, find the surprising angle. The counterintuitive insight. The overlooked detail that changes everything.

The window to a new world can also show you home. That's what Raymond Loewy created when he designed the NASA space orbital. In the most alien environment imaginable, he put a window. Familiarity in the midst of surprise.

What's one thing you're offering that might be too novel for your audience? How could you make it feel more familiar?
