For thousands of years, the smartest people in the world have been trying to crack the same code.

Why do we like what we like? Is there a formula for popularity?

Derek Thompson, author of "Hit Makers," gave a TEDx talk that flips everything you think you know about marketing.

Here is the counterintuitive truth: we do not actually like new things. We hate them.

The most common word in advertising is "new." Companies spend billions convincing us to crave the next big thing. But the science says the opposite. The mere-exposure effect, one of the oldest and most robust findings in psychology, proves that familiarity breeds preference. Not contempt. Preference.

Think about it. We seek out new songs, but the ones we love most have familiar chord structures. We want new movies, but every year the majority of top-grossing films are sequels, adaptations, or reboots. Familiar, familiar, familiar.

Thompson calls this the "familiarity principle." The things that become popular are not radically new. They are familiar with a twist. They give us the comfort of the known plus the thrill of the slightly different.

He calls this " MAYA: Most Advanced Yet Acceptable." That is the sweet spot. Too familiar and it is boring. Too novel and it is scary. The hits live right in between.

This is why the iPhone was not the first smartphone. It was the first smartphone that felt like something you already understood. It was a phone, an iPod, and an internet communicator. Three familiar things, combined in a new way.

This is why Star Wars was a Western in space. Why Harry Potter was a boarding school story with wizards. Why every breakthrough idea is usually a remix of what came before.

The implications for your business are massive.

If you are trying to sell something completely new, you are making it harder than it needs to be. People do not want to learn a new category. They want to understand how your thing fits into a category they already know.

Do not say "This is revolutionary and unlike anything you have ever seen." Say "This is like X, but for Y." Give them the familiar anchor, then show them the twist.

The most successful products, songs, movies, and ideas are not original in the way we think. They are original in the way they combine familiar elements. They feel fresh because of how the pieces are arranged, not because the pieces themselves are new.

So if you are building a brand, launching a product, or creating content, remember: novelty is overrated. Fluency is underrated. Make people feel at home first. Then surprise them.
