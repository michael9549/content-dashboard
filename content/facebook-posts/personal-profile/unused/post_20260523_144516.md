Michelle Cunningham has a recruiting strategy that actually works.

She says there are three reasons why people are super successful in network marketing. And they are not what most people think.

Reason one: they have a clear, simple system. Not a complicated one. A simple one. The people who recruit 20 people in 10 days do not have some secret magic. They have a process that anyone can follow. They know exactly what to say, when to say it, and who to say it to.

Reason two: they lead with the problem, not the opportunity. Most network marketers lead with "This is an amazing company with amazing products and you can make amazing money." That does not work because everyone has heard it before. The top recruiters lead with a problem their prospect already has. "Are you tired of trading time for money?" "Do you feel like your income has a ceiling?" "Would you like to build something that pays you even when you are not working?"

Reason three: they follow up faster than everyone else. Cunningham says speed is the separator. The person who follows up within an hour crushes the person who follows up within a day. The person who follows up within a day crushes the person who follows up within a week. Most people do not follow up at all. They send one message, get no response, and assume the person is not interested. The pros follow up five, seven, ten times. Not in an annoying way. In a persistent way.

Here is the part that most people miss: recruiting is not about convincing. It is about sorting. Your job is not to talk everyone into joining. Your job is to find the people who are already looking for something and show them that this might be it.

Cunningham's approach is refreshingly direct. She does not try to be everything to everyone. She finds people who have a specific problem and offers a specific solution. That is it.

If you are in network marketing and struggling to recruit, ask yourself: do you have a simple system that anyone can follow? Are you leading with problems or opportunities? And are you following up fast enough and long enough?

Most people fail at recruiting because they make it complicated. The pros make it simple.
