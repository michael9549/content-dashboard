I came across a video from Alex Hormozi breaking down the only three things you should say when closing a sale, and it completely shifted how I think about selling.

Most people think selling is about teaching, explaining features, and proving how much you know. That's actually the opposite of what works.

The more complex the sale, the more the prospect needs to come to the conclusion on their own that they should work with you. They can't feel forced or pushed. They need to break their own beliefs.

**The Three Types of Statements**

When you're selling, only three types of statements should come out of your mouth: questions, restatements, and short stories.

That's it. No long explanations. No teaching. No technobabble about your process or methodology.

**First: Questions**

Questions should be the most common thing you say. Leading questions that guide the prospect to the conclusion that you can solve their problem.

The types of questions that matter: Why are you here? What are you struggling with? Where are you right now? Where are you trying to go? What have you tried so far? How's that working for you? Why is this important within the bigger context of your life?

These aren't random. They're designed to get the prospect to confront their current reality and recognize the gap between where they are and where they want to be.

The person has to have their beliefs broken in order to take action. You're not providing value by teaching them tactics they won't execute. You're providing value by questioning their beliefs so they actually change.

Everyone gets benefits from breaking beliefs. Only 10% of people get benefits from tactics. So you're actually servicing 100% of people by focusing on beliefs rather than tactics that only one in ten might use.

**Second: Restatements**

After they say something, you restate it back to them. This shows you understand and you're listening.

"So what I'm hearing is this is the primary issue you've been trying to solve. This is what you've done so far, and this is why it hasn't worked. Does that sound about right?"

Notice this isn't a declarative statement of fact. You're not teaching. You're confirming understanding.

**Third: Short Stories**

When they ask how your program works, this is where most salespeople fail. They blab. They talk and talk about their system, their process, their methodology. They try to teach.

Instead, you tell short stories that illustrate what the prospect will experience as a result.

For example, if selling gym memberships: "Fundamentally, until you learn how to market your business, you're never going to be free. You said your goal is to be free, to have this business run on its own. Does that sound right?"

"Well, do you see a world where you can just offload lead generation and sales? What if that person goes out of business? You're never actually going to be free until you learn this skill. You'll have to confront this either today, tomorrow, or ten years from now. Does that make sense?"

You're telling a story to break a belief. Not explaining how you teach marketing.

Another example for weight loss: "Do you like cake? Cookies? Do you think you could never eat them for the rest of your life? No? So we need to figure out how you can eat what you enjoy and still lose weight. If you like the food more than what you're currently eating, you'll stick with it. Does that sound fair?"

Notice you didn't talk about meal plans, macros, or exercise selection. None of that matters in the conversation. You just need agreement on the approach.

**The Real Secret**

The best way to sell is to have the person feel like you understand them. The best way for someone to feel understood is for you to actually give a damn.

If you actually care, you'll know what questions to ask and in what order. Those are the questions you'd naturally ask someone you want to help.

Scripts were invented to mimic the structure of a conversation between someone who cares and someone they can help. That's what a sale is.

You're breaking their beliefs. You're coaching them to the conclusion they haven't been able to reach: that they need to work with you to solve their problem.

Approach it with the mindset that you want to provide value whether they work with you or not. This is still the solution they need. When you do this, your ego detaches from closing percentages. You start helping people. And by extension, you sell more.

Not by proving your expertise. Not by ego-babbling about how smart you are. But by asking questions, confirming understanding, and telling stories that break beliefs.

One big rule: don't pitch the program until you believe the person is ready to hear it.
