# 30 Years of Business Knowledge in One Core Idea

This video is a masterclass from someone who has built multiple successful businesses over three decades. The title says 30 years of knowledge in 2 hours and 26 minutes, but the core philosophy can be summarized in one sentence. Sell the sizzle, not the steak.

The speaker starts with a story about Steve Jobs. When Apple launched products, Jobs did not stand on stage listing specs. He did not talk about processors or memory or technical features. He talked about who the product was for. The game changers. The creative types. The people who think differently. He sold the identity, not the item.

This is the mistake most people make in sales and marketing. They focus on what the product does instead of who the customer becomes by using it. Nobody buys a drill because they want a drill. They buy it because they want a hole. And more importantly, they want to be the kind of person who can fix things themselves.

The speaker breaks down sales into three steps that sound simple but almost nobody follows.

Step one. Do they need you? Most people skip this entirely. They try to sell to everyone instead of finding the people who actually have the problem they solve. The speaker says he gets emails all the time from people who clearly have not researched what he does. They say they love his work but have no idea what his work actually is. These emails get ignored. But when someone clearly understands his business and explains exactly how they can add value, he pays attention.

Step two. Do you like each other? This is where most business advice falls apart. People think sales is about tactics and scripts. The speaker says if you do not genuinely like the person and they do not genuinely like you, the deal should not happen. Even if it does, you will end up working with someone you cannot stand. He says watch how they treat the waiter. If they are rude to service staff, drop them. It is not worth the revenue.

Step three. The deal happens naturally. If you have done steps one and two right, the deal almost closes itself. He says he has done enormous deals where he named his price and the client came back offering more because they wanted to make sure the relationship worked. That only happens when there is genuine mutual respect and need.

The video also covers what separates the top 1% of salespeople from everyone else. Harvard did a study on the best salespeople in the world. The top 50 would approach a prospect about five times before giving up. But the top 1% did something completely different. They built systems to stay in touch for years.

The speaker says when he started his company, he wrote down 50 companies he wanted to work with. From day one, he built a system to reach out to them every single month. Not to sell. Just to build the relationship. At Christmas he sent cards. At Chinese New Year he sent something. When he learned something useful about their industry, he shared it. Some clients came quickly. Others took nine years. But he got almost all of them because he played the long game.

The final insight is about marketing. The speaker says marketing should be fun. He tells a story about buying a staircase that was for sale in London for £26,000. It made no logical sense. But he knew it symbolized something important for his business. Step by step, you can get anywhere. He turned it into a marketing campaign that got him on the front page of the New York Times, the BBC, and newspapers worldwide. Millions of dollars in free coverage because he did something memorable and fun.

The lesson is not to buy a staircase. The lesson is that marketing works when it is authentic and enjoyable. If you are forcing yourself to do TikTok because someone said you should, but you hate being on camera, it will not work. Pick the marketing that fits your personality. If you love writing, do email and LinkedIn. If you love video, do YouTube. The sustainability matters more than the platform.

What is your staircase? What is the thing that makes you stand out and that you can build a story around?

Which of these three sales steps do you think most people skip?
