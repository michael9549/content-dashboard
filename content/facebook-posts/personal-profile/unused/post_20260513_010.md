I came across a training from Jeremy Miner about handling sales objections, and his approach to getting prospects out of their own mental frames really changed how I think about the sales process.

Most salespeople dread objections. "I don't have the money." "Will it work for me?" "I need to think it over." "I'll do it down the road." These are the frames prospects bring into conversations.

Until you learn how to get them out of their frame, they'll keep giving you these objections over and over. And responding with rebuttals, telling them why they're wrong, rarely works.

**The Problem With Traditional Approaches**

When you get an email saying "we really liked you, but now's not a good time, we'll get back to you," what do most salespeople do? They call back with rebuttals. They try to convince the prospect they're wrong.

Sometimes it works. It's a numbers game. But it's rough. Not very fun. And not very effective.

**Understanding Why Prospects Object**

Here's the fundamental issue: if your prospect feels no need to change, that's why they give you objections. Even though they raised their hand for help, even though they booked a call on your calendar, they're not feeling enough pain or fear to actually take action.

The two biggest emotional drivers that cause humans to change are pain and the fear of future pain. If you can't help them relive the pain of their current situation and feel fear about what happens if they don't solve it, they feel no need to change.

**Going Six Layers Deep**

Surface-level problems don't drive action. You have to go deeper.

A health coach might help entrepreneurs with low energy and brain fog. But what's the consequence if they don't fix it? They won't have energy to dominate their business. They'll lose money and productivity. The business won't perform as well. Eventually, the business might fail. Then what happens to them? To their family?

See how you can go six layers deeper? That's where the sale is made. Not on the surface level about getting healthier. It's about the consequences of not fixing the problem. It's the fear of what the future looks like if they don't solve it.

**The Reframing Technique**

When a prospect says "now's not a good time, we'll get back to you," you need to take them out of that frame. Here's how it works.

"Can I ask you something? How can I communicate to you that you might be making a mistake without you getting upset with me?"

This is a consequence question that deframes them. It takes them out of their current thinking. Notice the concerned tone. That tone seeds doubt. It suggests you know something they don't. It triggers their brain to lower their guard and become more open.

**Layering Multiple Frames**

Once you've started the deframing process, you loop back and create new frames. You ask questions that make them confront the reality of their situation.

"What happens if you don't do anything about this and your performance keeps declining? How long is your boss going to give you before he lets you go? What's going to happen to you and your family at that point?"

"How does your spouse feel about you continuing to have these issues? Does she want you to keep going through this?"

"Why look at doing this now? Why not push it down the road like a lot of executives who never end up getting promoted because they never get their health back?"

**Identity Framing**

That last question is an identity frame. You're getting them to push back against being the type of person who keeps having that problem. Does he want to be like those executives who never fix their issues? You're getting him to run away from that identity.

**The Pattern**

First, you deframe with a consequence question that creates doubt and opens their mind. Then you layer multiple frames that make them confront the pain of their current situation and the fear of future consequences. You use identity frames to get them to reject the type of person who stays stuck.

You're not telling them what to do. You're asking questions that lead them to persuade themselves. They have to feel the pain. They have to see the future consequences. They have to decide they don't want to be that person who keeps procrastinating.

**Why It Works**

Traditional objection handling tries to logic people out of their position. Reframing works with human psychology. People make decisions based on emotion and justify with logic later.

When you help prospects relive their current pain and feel fear about future pain, you create emotional urgency. When you use identity frames, you tap into their desire to see themselves a certain way.

The sale happens when the prospect persuades themselves. Your job is to ask the questions that make that self-persuasion possible.
