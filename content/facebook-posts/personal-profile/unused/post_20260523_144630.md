Dan Martell has been selling software since he was 17 years old. That is 27 years of sales experience, from entry-level deals to enterprise contracts with companies like Procter and Gamble and Johnson and Johnson.

He says most entrepreneurs are terrible at sales. Not because they lack talent, but because they think sales is about talking. It is not. Sales is about listening.

Martell's first insight: the best salespeople ask better questions. Most people ask surface-level questions like "What do you need?" or "What is your budget?" The pros ask questions that uncover the real problem behind the problem. "What happens if this issue is still unresolved six months from now?" "How is this affecting your team morale?" "What is this costing you in lost revenue or missed opportunities?"

His second insight: silence is a weapon. After you ask a powerful question, shut up. Most people get uncomfortable with silence and start talking to fill the gap. Martell says the person who breaks the silence first loses. Let your prospect sit with the question. Let them think. Let them feel the weight of their own answer. That is where the sale happens.

His third insight: objections are buying signals. When someone says "I need to think about it" or "It is too expensive," they are not rejecting you. They are asking for help justifying the decision. Martell says your job is not to overcome objections. Your job is to understand what is behind them. "Too expensive" usually means "I do not see the value yet." "Need to think about it" usually means "I am not convinced this will work for me." Address the underlying fear, not the surface objection.

His fourth insight: follow up or fail. Martell says 80% of sales happen after the fifth follow-up. Most entrepreneurs give up after one or two. They send a proposal, hear nothing, and assume the person is not interested. The pros have a systematic follow-up process that keeps them top of mind without being annoying.

The bottom line: sales is a skill, not a gift. Martell was not born a great salesperson. He learned by doing, failing, adjusting, and doing again. If you are an entrepreneur who struggles with sales, the good news is that it is learnable. The bad news is that you cannot avoid it forever.
