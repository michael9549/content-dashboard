This TEDx talk on belief and mindset referenced Josh Waitzkin, the chess prodigy from Searching for Bobby Fischer.

Waitzkin won his first national chess championship at age 9. But he says the best thing that ever happened to him was losing his first championship. Because it helped him avoid the psychological trap of believing he was special, smarter than everyone else, that success was inevitable because of some fixed ability.

Carol Dweck's research at Stanford backs this up. She identified two mindsets: fixed (intelligence is static) and growth (intelligence can be developed). She tracked hundreds of seventh graders for two years. The students with growth mindsets, who believed they could change their intelligence, improved their grades over time. Those with fixed mindsets didn't.

The performance gap widened over time. Not because of effort alone, but because of the underlying belief about whether effort mattered.

The key insight: when we try to build grit or perseverance directly, it's less effective than targeting the mindset that produces them. The belief comes first. The behavior follows.
