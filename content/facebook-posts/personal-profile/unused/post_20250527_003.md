What if I told you that every prospect you talk to already has a belief system programmed into them before you even open your mouth?

Jeremy Miner studied psychology in university, and he dropped something that completely shifted how I think about sales.

Every human being has what psychologists call a "frame." It's their worldview. Their belief system. The lens through which they see everything.

And here's the kicker: most of these beliefs were installed when they were children. Between ages 4 and 13. By their parents. Their religion. Their friends. The media they consumed.

Think about that. The person sitting across from you in a sales conversation isn't making objective decisions. They're running software that was programmed decades ago.

So what does this mean for you as a salesperson?

It means your prospect already has pre-wired responses to sales situations. When a salesperson starts talking, what do most people immediately think?

"How much is this going to cost?"

"They're going to try to pressure me."

"I need to be careful or I'll get ripped off."

These aren't conscious thoughts. They're automatic. Installed by society, past experiences, and every bad sales interaction they've ever had.

Your job isn't to overcome these beliefs with logic and features. Your job is to understand the frame they're operating from and work within it.

Miner talks about how mastering this understanding of human psychology can 10x your income. Not because you're manipulating people. But because you're finally speaking their language instead of yours.

The best salespeople don't fight the frame. They enter it. They understand the belief system first, then guide the prospect to a new conclusion that fits within their existing worldview.

Stop trying to convince people they're wrong about salespeople. Stop overcoming objections with clever rebuttals. Start understanding the frame they're bringing to the conversation.

What belief systems do you think your prospects have about your industry before they ever talk to you?
