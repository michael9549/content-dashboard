I used to enter trades based on indicators.

RSI was oversold? Buy. MACD crossed? Enter. Stochastic in the zone? Time to trade.

I had all the tools. I knew all the patterns. And I was consistently losing money.

Then I stopped looking at indicators and started looking at price. Everything changed.

**Why Indicators Fail**

Indicators aren't magic. They're just math applied to price. Lagging math, usually.

By the time your moving average crosses, the move has already happened. By the time RSI hits overbought, price might be ready to keep running. Indicators tell you what price already did, not what it's going to do.

Worse, indicators distract you from what actually matters: market structure.

**What Is Market Structure?**

Market structure is the story price is telling you. It's the pattern of highs and lows that reveals whether buyers or sellers are in control.

In an uptrend, price makes higher highs and higher lows. Each pullback stops above the previous low. Buyers are aggressive enough to push price to new highs, and they're defending their positions on pullbacks.

In a downtrend, price makes lower lows and lower highs. Each rally fails below the previous high. Sellers are in control, and they're selling every bounce.

When structure breaks—when a higher low gets violated in an uptrend, or a lower high gets taken out in a downtrend—the trend is likely changing.

This isn't theory. This is how markets actually work.

**The Three Market Phases**

Every market is always in one of three phases:

**Trending**: Price is moving directionally with conviction. Higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower lows and lower highs (downtrend). This is where the best opportunities exist, trading in the direction of the trend.

**Ranging**: Price is moving sideways between support and resistance. No clear direction. Higher lows and lower highs—conflicting signals. This is where most traders lose money trying to predict breakouts that never come.

**Transitioning**: Structure is breaking down. A trending market is showing signs of exhaustion, or a ranging market is building energy for a breakout. These are the hardest phases to trade because direction is unclear.

**Reading Structure in Real-Time**

Here's how I analyze any chart in 30 seconds:

1. **Identify the trend**: Are we making higher highs/lows (up), lower highs/lows (down), or neither (range)?

2. **Locate key levels**: Where are the recent swing highs and lows? These are the boundaries that matter.

3. **Watch for structure breaks**: Is price respecting the structure, or is it breaking down?

4. **Determine your bias**: Only trade in the direction of the structure. If it's unclear, stay out.

**The Power of Multi-Timeframe Analysis**

Structure on one timeframe might look completely different on another. A messy range on the 15-minute chart might be a clean pullback in a daily uptrend.

My rule: Always know the structure on the daily and 4-hour charts before trading lower timeframes. The higher timeframe structure determines your bias. The lower timeframe helps with entry timing.

If the daily is in an uptrend, I'm only looking for long setups on the 1-hour. If the daily is ranging, I'm more cautious or staying out entirely.

**Structure-Based Trading Plan**

Here's a simple framework you can use:

**In an uptrend**: Look to buy pullbacks to support (previous resistance turned support, or the most recent higher low). Place stops below the structure. Target the next resistance level or let winners run.

**In a downtrend**: Look to sell rallies to resistance (previous support turned resistance, or the most recent lower high). Place stops above the structure. Target the next support level.

**In a range**: Wait. Or if you must trade, buy support and sell resistance with tight stops. Know that you're counter-trend and size accordingly.

**When structure breaks**: This is often the best opportunity. A break of structure signals a potential new trend. Wait for the retest of the broken level, then enter in the direction of the new trend.

**Why This Works**

Market structure works because it reflects the actual behavior of market participants. It's not a prediction—it's an observation of what's actually happening.

When buyers are in control, they create higher highs and higher lows. You don't need an indicator to tell you this. You can see it.

When that pattern breaks, it means something has shifted. Maybe buyers are exhausted. Maybe sellers have stepped in. The structure tells you the story.

**The Discipline Required**

Reading structure is simple. Trading it consistently is hard.

It requires patience to wait for the right setup. It requires discipline to stay out when structure is unclear. It requires confidence to hold through pullbacks that don't violate structure.

Most traders fail not because they can't read charts, but because they can't control their urge to trade. They see a moving average cross and they jump in, ignoring that price is making lower highs in a downtrend.

Don't be that trader. Let structure be your guide. It's the closest thing to a compass the market offers.
