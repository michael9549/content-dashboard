Why do we like what we like?

Derek Thompson, author of "Hit Makers," just gave a TED Talk that answers this question with a four-letter code. And it's the key to selling anything.

Here's the paradox: advertisers think we crave novelty. The most common word in advertising history? "New."

But we don't actually like novelty. We hate it.

Psychologists have known this for decades. It's called the mere-exposure effect. The more we're exposed to something, the more we like it. Familiarity breeds preference, not contempt.

So if we hate new things, why do we ever buy them?

Enter Raymond Loewy, the father of industrial design. He designed the Lucky Strike pack, the Greyhound bus, the Air Force One livery. Everything he touched turned to gold.

His secret? He called it MAYA: Most Advanced Yet Acceptable.

People want things that are new, but not too new. Advanced, but familiar. Different, but recognizable.

Think about the iPhone. It was revolutionary, but it looked like something you already understood: a phone. The iPod: "1,000 songs in your pocket" â a new concept, but you knew what songs were and what pockets were.

This is why most innovations fail. They're too novel. Too scary. Too different from what people already know and trust.

The sweet spot? Make it 90% familiar, 10% new.

When you're selling, remember this. Your prospect wants improvement, not revolution. They want better, not completely different. They want to feel smart for buying, not scared for trying something weird.

Frame your offer as the natural next step, not a giant leap into the unknown.

Most Advanced Yet Acceptable. That's the code.

How can you make what you're selling feel more familiar while still being better?
