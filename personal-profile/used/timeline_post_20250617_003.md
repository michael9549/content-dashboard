This breakdown of entrepreneurship types clarified something I hadn't fully considered.

There are two fundamentally different kinds:

Small-medium enterprise entrepreneurship: the dry cleaner, the restaurant, the local service business. Linear growth. Serves a local need well. Important to the economy, but designed to stay small. Cash flow is steady but limited by geography.

Innovation-driven entrepreneurship: global or super-regional markets. Exponential growth potential. Requires more capital upfront. Loses money initially, but if the innovation works, it scales because the underlying market is essentially unlimited.

The distinction matters because most advice about entrepreneurship assumes one or the other. The local business owner needs different strategies than the founder building for global scale. Their cash flow patterns are opposites. Their capital needs are opposites. Their definition of success is different.

What's interesting: both are valid. Neither is better. But confusing the two leads to bad decisions. Taking local business advice and applying it to a scalable venture, or vice versa, creates mismatched expectations.

The key question isn't "should I be an entrepreneur?" It's: which type am I building, and does my strategy match that reality?
