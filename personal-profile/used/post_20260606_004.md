I built and sold three companies in under ten years for millions of dollars. They scaled so fast it felt like cheating.

One of my latest companies hit $2 million a month in under 18 months. Today I own a portfolio of companies on track to become worth $1 billion in less than three years.

Here is what I learned about speed. Most people are moving in slow motion because they are focused on the wrong things.

**Focus on cash, not perfection.**

Stop trying to make everything perfect and just start selling. Everybody wants to start a business, but nobody wants to start selling. I always say the moment you sell something to a stranger is the moment you become an entrepreneur.

The more money you can get in cash upfront before you spend a lot of money to deliver what you sold, the easier it is to grow. There is a massive difference between receivables and revenue. Cash in the bank gives you leverage. Invoices give you anxiety.

Perfection is the enemy of profit. Your website does not need to be beautiful. Your product does not need every feature. Your funnel does not need ten upsells. You need one offer that solves one problem for one person. Get it in front of people. Let them pay you. Then improve based on what they tell you.

**Speed comes from saying no.**

Most businesses die from indigestion, not starvation. They try to serve everyone, offer everything, and be everywhere. The result is mediocrity across the board.

The fastest-growing companies I have built all had one thing in common. We were incredibly focused. One offer. One avatar. One channel. One metric that mattered.

Every time you say yes to something that is not core to your business, you are saying no to the thing that actually moves the needle. The opportunity cost of distraction is the highest cost in business.

**Hire slow, fire fast.**

Your first ten hires determine your culture. Get this wrong and you will spend years fixing it. Take your time finding people who align with your vision and values. But when someone is not working out, act quickly. A bad hire does not just underperform. They drain energy, create drama, and pull good people down with them.

The best teams are small, aligned, and obsessed. Not big, bloated, and bureaucratic.

**The bottom line.**

Speed in business is not about working harder. It is about making better decisions faster. It is about focusing on what generates cash today, not what might work someday. It is about saying no to almost everything so you can say yes to the few things that actually matter.

If you are starting from zero, you have an advantage. You have no legacy systems to maintain. No bad hires to manage. No complex processes to unwind. You can move fast because you are light.

Use that advantage before you lose it.

What is one thing you could stop doing today that would let you move faster on what actually matters?
