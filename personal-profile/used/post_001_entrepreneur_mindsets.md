I watched this TED talk the other day about what actually separates successful entrepreneurs from the ones who fail.

And honestly? It's not what most people think.

It's not about having the perfect business plan. It's not about raising a ton of money. It's not even about sticking to your "core competencies" like they teach in every business school.

It's about having the right MINDSET.

Here are the 6 mindsets that successful entrepreneurs share (and why they break all the traditional business rules):

1. **"Yes, we can" instead of "That's not what we do."** When a customer asks for something outside your expertise, don't say no. Figure it out. One Brazilian entrepreneur reinvented his entire business FOUR TIMES by saying yes to customer requests he had no idea how to fulfill. Today his company is worth millions.

2. **Focus on problems, not products.** Big companies obsess over tweaking products (new Tide! New Coke!). Entrepreneurs obsess over solving real problems. A guy named Jonathan Thorne built a medical device company by focusing on ONE problem: surgical forceps that stick to human tissue. Sold it to Stryker for a fortune.

3. **Think narrow, not broad.** Nike didn't start by trying to make shoes for everyone. They made shoes for ONE group: elite distance runners. Once they nailed that, THEN they expanded. Start small. Dominate it. Then grow.

4. **Ask for cash BEFORE you build.** Tesla sold 100 Roadsters for $100,000 each before building car #1. That's $10 million in the bank, zero risk. When they launched the Model 3, they collected $1,000 deposits from half a million people. Half a BILLION dollars before production started. Get paid to build your business.

5. **Borrow everything you can.** Go Ape, a treetop adventure company, didn't buy land. They partnered with the UK Forestry Commission and borrowed the trees, the parking lots, the bathrooms. All they added was their course equipment. Today they have 30+ locations. Minimize your investment. Maximize your leverage.

6. **Don't ask for permission.** Uber didn't ask San Francisco regulators if they could start a taxi company with no taxis. They just did it. Sometimes the rules are outdated. Sometimes they don't apply to what's possible today. Move fast. Figure out the rules later.

The point?

Stop following the "best practices" that work for big, slow companies.

Break the rules that hold you back. Build something people actually need. And stop waiting for someone to tell you it's okay to start.

You already have permission. Now GO.
